Applying NLP LS approaches to Chinese text

To recap from Part 1, I'm going to explore traditional NLP methods for LS to approach my project problem of simplifying online Chinese text. While NLP has been revolutionized by the use of large language models (LLMs) like GPT, previous NLP techniques could still be useful to our problem. In particular, these approaches can be much less computationally intensive than running text through a LLM with more than 100 million parameters, and could provide acceptable simplification results if the input sentence is not too complex in syntactic structure.

The typical process for LS is shown in the image below. To summarize, we (1) identify complex words in the sentence, (2) find candidate words with similar meanings to the complex word, (3) rank the candidate words, and (4) replace the word in the sentence to form the simplified sentence.

For many languages, LS requires performing a morphological analysis of the complex word, either through computational or rule-based methods, so that the replaced simple word matches the original word tense and context. For example, the simplication of "The horse galloped to the stables" should read "The horse ran to the stables" rather than "runs" or "running". In this regard, Chinese grammar is very simple: verb characters are never modified directly, and there are no special conjugation rules that alter a word based on tense, speaker, or case. However, some Chinese characters can change meaning based on context, or the figurative use-case may be different than the literal. With that said, let's dive in to the process by first identifying complex words.

Recognizing complex Chinese words and Named Entities

For the simplest LS (see image above), it is sufficient in Chinese to find a synonym simple word to replace a complex word. Therefore, my first task is (1) identifying complex words, and (2) identifying synonyms.

Chinese language ability is official evaluated with the HSK (Hanyu Shuiping Kaoshi - Chinese proficiency exam). The latest set of HSK vocabulary has cumulative levels from 1 to 9 (HSK1 being beginner level) with a total of about 11,000 vocabulary words. Because Chinese learners -- including myself -- often use the HSK system as both a study tool and a proficiency goal, I aim to specifically focus on HSK-level vocabulary in my TS/LS approach.
